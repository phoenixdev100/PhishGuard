    Process

    How PhishGuard Works

    Three-stage analysis combining AI, real-time lookups, and battle-tested heuristics.

    Step 01

    URL & Domain Parsing

    The URL is broken down into its components — scheme, domain, path, query. We check length, special characters, subdomains, suspicious TLDs, and encoded characters immediately.

    Step 02

    Machine Learning Model

    30 hand-crafted features are fed into a trained classifier. The model was trained on thousands of phishing and legitimate URLs to identify subtle statistical patterns.

    Step 03

    Risk Score & Report

    All signals are merged into a 0–100 risk score. SSL validity, domain age (WHOIS), and network behaviour are factored in for a final verdict with a detailed breakdown.

    Capabilities

    What We Analyse

    Every URL scan runs through six independent checks for maximum coverage.

    AI / ML Detection

    Trained classifier on 30 URL features including structure, encoding, and host anomalies.

    SSL Certificate Check

    Validates HTTPS presence and certificate authenticity to flag unencrypted or spoofed sites.

    Domain Age (WHOIS)

    Newly registered domains are a red flag. We query WHOIS records to check domain creation date.

    DNS Resolution

    Verifies that the domain actually resolves. Ghost domains and unregistered names are flagged instantly.

    Redirect Chain Analysis

    Follows HTTP redirects and flags chains of 3 or more hops, a common phishing obfuscation trick.

    Page Content Scan

    Inspects favicon, forms, inline scripts, and external resource ratios of the live page source.

    Threat Intel

    Types of Threats We Catch

    PhishGuard is trained to recognise all major categories of malicious URLs in the wild.

    Phishing Attacks

    Fake login pages that impersonate banks, payment gateways, or social platforms to steal credentials. Often use similar-looking domain names and copied branding.

    Malware Distribution

    URLs that trigger automatic downloads of viruses, trojans, ransomware, or spyware through drive-by downloads or deceptive download buttons.

    Redirect Loops

    Chains of redirects designed to confuse analysis tools. The final destination may be a phishing page or exploit kit that bypasses simple URL checkers.

    Social Engineering

    Pages with urgent messaging, fake warnings, or prize notifications designed to manipulate users into entering sensitive data or calling rogue support numbers.
